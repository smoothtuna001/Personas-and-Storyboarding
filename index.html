Observe

Over the course of a few days, I watched people use the waste receptacle interface, iteracting with it in a variety of ways. In general users spent under 10 seconds disposing of their item, and did not pay the receptacle much attention unless the opening was jammed or dirty. Very few users used the foot pedal to open the receptacle, instead opting to use the pull-down handle.

Interview

Questions Asked:

  • Describe your ideal waste receptacle.
  • What types of challenges do you experience when using a Bigbelly trash or recycling bin?
  • When going to throw something out, how do you open the receptacle?
    • I use the handle
    • I use the foot pedal
    • It depends on...
  • How often have you used these bins in the past two weeks?
    • Once
    • A few times
    • Regularly
    • Daily

Pain Points:
  • Small area to insert, can’t fit anything oversized.
  • Foot pedal puts you off balance and often stuff is stuck in so you can’t open
  • Over-reliant on software working to notify waste management on when to empty the receptacles

Successes:
  • Keeps rodents and other unwanted guests from going through trash
  • Compactor is better for the environment
  • No waste sight or smell

Personas

Based on observations and interviews, I created three personas (fictional users) that bridge the different types of users that interact with the waste receptacle. Personas are valuable because they help designers employ empathy skills to design for specific people as opposed to trying to imagine features for a wide range of potential users.

Storyboard

After generating a few personas to exemplify different use cases of my interface, I created a storyboard to show one specific interaction that I could imagine my persona having in the real world. The storyboard is made from the observation and interview stages, and is helpful in exploring the relative potential success of the product features of the interface.
